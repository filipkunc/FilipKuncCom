Years ago, while building [MeshMaker](https://filipkunc.com/meshmaker), I added a little corner I called the OpenCLPlayground.
The mesh editor already drew triangles through the graphics pipeline the usual way.
What I wanted to poke at was the other pipeline, the general-purpose one, where the GPU is just a wide pile of arithmetic and not a triangle-drawing machine.

The toy problem I picked was recomputing vertex normals.
It was never about the normals themselves.
It was about getting data onto the compute side of the GPU, doing something with it, and handing the result straight to the renderer without dragging it back through the CPU.

This post is that experiment, redone with the tools of 2026.
Here it is, running in your browser.

<ClothLab client:visible />

That is a cloth, simulated and shaded entirely on the GPU.
It moves every frame, so its normals cannot be precomputed and baked once.
They are rebuilt from scratch each frame, and the slider runs that past a million triangles.
Drag the resolution up and the GPU stays smooth.
Flip the normals over to the CPU and watch the same work fall apart.

The rest of the post is how this is put together, and then the same little kernel benchmarked across WGSL, CUDA, OpenCL, and Slang.

## What a normal is, and why recompute it

A triangle has one normal, the direction it faces, and you get it from the cross product of two of its edges.
Smooth shading needs a normal per vertex instead, so a shared vertex looks rounded rather than faceted.
The standard recipe is to average the face normals of every triangle that touches the vertex, weighted by area.
That is the whole computation, and it is the same handful of lines whether it runs on a CPU core or ten thousand GPU lanes.

Here it is on a static shape, so you can see what the normals actually are.

<NormalsLab client:visible />

That icosahedron has twelve vertices and twenty flat faces.
Smooth per-vertex normals make it read as a rounded ball, and the orange lines are the normals themselves.
For a shape like this the normals never change, so you would compute them once and forget about them.
That is exactly why a static mesh is the wrong demo for the GPU.

## The same thing on the GPU

The CPU version scatters: it loops over triangles and adds into vertices.
On the GPU that is a trap.
Thousands of triangle threads would write the same vertex at once, and that is a data race.
The obvious fix is an atomic add, except WGSL has no atomics on floats, so you would have to integer-encode each component and decode it in a second pass.

So I flipped the loop.
Instead of one thread per triangle scattering outward, the GPU runs one thread per vertex that gathers inward.
Each thread walks only the triangles touching its own vertex and writes its own slot exactly once.
No contention, no atomics.
It needs a small vertex-to-triangle table built once on the CPU, which is cheap.

## Why the cloth, and not the icosahedron

A static mesh hides the whole point.
Its normals are computed once, so it does not matter whether that happens on the CPU, on the GPU, or by hand in advance.
The moment the geometry moves every frame, the normals have to be rebuilt every frame, and now the cost is real and recurring.

A cloth is the cleanest example I know.
It is a grid of particles pulled on by gravity and wind, held together by distance constraints, and it never stops moving.
The whole thing runs on the GPU as a sequence of compute passes.

First a Verlet integration step moves every free particle.

<Excerpt source="src/lib/gpu-cloth/shaders/cloth.compute.wgsl" region="cloth-integrate" lang="wgsl" />

Then the distance constraints pull neighbours back toward their rest length.
This has the same write-race problem as the scatter normals did: a constraint touches two particles, and neighbouring constraints share particles.
The fix is to color the constraints so that no two in the same pass ever touch the same particle, then run one color at a time.
For a grid the coloring is just parity, so the whole solve is eight small passes with no atomics.

<Excerpt source="src/lib/gpu-cloth/shaders/cloth.compute.wgsl" region="cloth-solve" lang="wgsl" />

One catch shows up only at scale.
Distance constraints pass tension between neighbours one step at a time, so on a small sheet a handful of solver passes reach all the way to the pins, but on a million-particle sheet they fall far short and the lower half sags away as if it were heavier.
The fix is a long-range attachment: cap each particle's distance to its nearest pin directly, using the taut-path length to that pin.
That holds the whole sheet up in a single pass, no matter how many particles there are, so the cloth behaves the same at every resolution.

<Excerpt source="src/lib/gpu-cloth/shaders/cloth.compute.wgsl" region="cloth-lra" lang="wgsl" />

And then the normals, recomputed from the freshly moved positions with the very same gather kernel from above.

## One buffer, two pipelines

This is the part I was chasing back in the MeshMaker days.

<DiagramPipelines />

When the compute passes finish, the new positions and normals are already sitting in GPU buffers.
The render pipeline takes those same buffers as its vertex inputs and draws them in place.
There is no map, no copy, no trip through CPU memory between simulating the cloth and drawing it.

<Excerpt source="src/lib/gpu-cloth/shaders/cloth.render.wgsl" region="cloth-shading" lang="wgsl" />

The buffers carry two usages at once, storage for the compute passes and vertex data for the render pass, and the GPU just reads them where they lie.
That hand-off is the real product of the general-purpose pipeline.
You are not borrowing the GPU to compute a number and bring it home.
You are computing it where it will be consumed.

## The bandwidth you do not spend

Switch the demo's normals from the GPU to the CPU and the frame rate collapses, especially as you raise the resolution.
The simulation still runs on the GPU, but now every frame has to copy the positions back to the CPU, recompute the normals in JavaScript, and upload them again.
The reading on the demo shows the bytes moving and the milliseconds spent.

That readback is the cost the GPU path never pays.
On a discrete card the buffers live across the PCIe bus, and pulling them back to the CPU means a real transfer plus the latency of waiting for the queue to drain.
At a million triangles that is megabytes a frame, every frame, in each direction.
Keeping the result on the GPU, the way the compute-to-render hand-off does, is how you avoid the whole bill.

Set them next to the [WGSL above](#the-same-thing-on-the-gpu) and they are the same idea four times.
Loop over the incident triangles, cross two edges, accumulate, normalize.
What differs between these worlds is the host code around the kernel, how you allocate and bind buffers, and whether the result can be handed to a renderer without a copy.
The arithmetic in the middle is boringly portable.

[Slang](https://shader-slang.org/) is the interesting one.
It is not a runtime, it is a language: it moved from NVIDIA to the [Khronos Group](https://www.khronos.org/) in late 2024, and one Slang source compiles to SPIR-V for Vulkan, MSL for Metal, HLSL for Direct3D, CUDA, and PTX.
The benchmark below compiles it to PTX and launches it through the CUDA driver.

So I ran all of them, on the same mesh, on my own machine, and let them race.
Each one compiles and runs the kernel two hundred times and reports its best.
They all produce the same normals, which the harness checks with a shared checksum, so the only thing that varies is speed.

<BenchTable />

The numbers are what you would expect and still fun to see.
A single CPU thread is the slowest by a wide margin.
The four GPU paths land within a hair of each other, because they are all compiling nearly the same kernel down to the same hardware.
WGSL, the one that also runs in the browser demo above, is right there with native CUDA.

The whole table is reproducible.
`npm run bench` generates the shared mesh, builds each kernel with whatever toolchains are installed, runs them, and writes the numbers this table reads.
Anything it cannot build, it skips out loud rather than quietly dropping.

## Then and now

The OpenCLPlayground in MeshMaker was a side experiment that never shipped.
Setting up an OpenCL context, sharing a buffer with OpenGL, and keeping the two pipelines in step was enough friction that it stayed a playground.

The friction is mostly gone now.
WebGPU puts the compute pipeline, the render pipeline, and the shared buffer behind one API, in the browser, with no driver to install.
The thing I wanted years ago, computing mesh data on the general-purpose side and handing it straight to the renderer, is now a cloth you can throw a million triangles at from your phone.
